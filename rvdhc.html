Preventing food waste

Not many people will be surprised to hear that food waste is a serious problem. In their efforts to improve the situation the municipalities of Den Bosch and Meierijstad in collaboration with external domain experts have developed an action plan to combat the issue and are willing to serve as a "test bed" in the Netherlands. I was commissioned to investigate the effect of their methods, review the progress and help formulate a strategy going forward.

Note: though this project is under an NDA, the municipalities have allowed me to (broadly) discuss aspects relating them and their data. The entire project spanned more research areas, those will largely be ignored here.

Findings

Using data from the several parties whilst comparing to a newly created questionnaire, I identified several examples where a misunderstanding of psychological effects would lead to suboptimal results. First of all, the definition of "food waste" was not clear to everybody, resulting in a mismatch between what domain experts believed to constitute food waste and what citizens considered as food waste (the latter being much more restrictive). Policies where being considered by the municipalities to combat what the people would not even see as food waste!

Secondly, we analysed what drives people to try to limit food waste, and what would prevent them from acting so? Even though generally people state that the two most important reasons are time and convenience, in analysing their behaviour we noted that pricing was by far the most significant factor in their decision-making. Somewhat counter-intuitively though, we also found that - given that a price increase would be inevitable - people are mostly fine with a flat, general increase as opposed to a quantity based one following the "the pollutor pays" principle.
